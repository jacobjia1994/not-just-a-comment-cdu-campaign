Bystander toolkit

You do not need the perfect sentence.

You need one safe first step, a way to support the person affected, and enough follow-through to show the room that racism will not be normalised.

1

Notice

Listen for jokes, myths, colour-blind comments, stereotypes and questions that make one student carry cultural education.

2

Check safety

Choose a response that does not increase risk for the person targeted. You can act in the moment, redirect the room, seek help or follow up later.

3

Name the pattern

Name the joke, stereotype, assumption, burden or silence. Avoid turning the moment into a debate about whether someone is a "bad person".

4

Redirect the room

Return the group to the task, suggest a First Nations-led resource, or ask staff for support if the setting feels unsafe.

5

Check in privately

Ask the person affected what they need. Do not ask them to reassure you, teach you or approve your response.

6

Follow through

Write down what happened if it is safe. Seek advice. Respect the affected person's choice about reporting.

Phrase bank

Short lines are easier to use under pressure.

Pause the moment

"Can we pause for a second?"

"I do not think that joke lands well."

"That sounded like a stereotype."

Shift the frame

"Support programs are about equity."

"Fairness includes noticing racism."

"No report does not mean no harm."

Reduce the burden

"Let's not put one person on the spot."

"We can do the reading instead of asking one student to explain."

"I can help find a First Nations-led resource."

Check in

"I noticed what happened. Are you okay?"

"Would you like me to stay with you?"

"Do you want help finding support, or would you prefer space?"

After the moment

Do not turn your response into another burden.

The aim is not to perform being a good bystander. The aim is to reduce harm, support the person affected and change the pattern that made the moment possible.

Do not ask for reassurance

Avoid making the person affected comfort you or rate your response.

Do not force a report

Offer options and support. Reporting should not remove control from the person harmed.

Do keep learning

Use First Nations-led resources. Do not rely on one student to explain racism, culture or history.
