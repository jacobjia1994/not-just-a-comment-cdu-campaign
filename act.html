Bystander toolkit

You do not need the perfect sentence.

You need one safe first step, a way to support the person affected, and a willingness to reset the peer norm.

1

Notice

Pay attention to jokes, assumptions, silence and "just asking" questions that place the burden on First Nations students.

2

Check safety

Choose a response that does not escalate risk for the person targeted. Direct action is not always the safest option.

3

Name it

Use plain language. You can name a stereotype, a racist joke, a harmful assumption or an unfair burden.

4

Redirect

If the room is unsafe, interrupt the flow, change the task, move the person away or ask a staff member for help.

5

Check in

Privately ask the person affected what they need. Do not demand a response or make them manage your feelings.

6

Follow through

Document safely, seek advice, report when appropriate and keep improving the norm after the moment passes.

Phrase bank

Short lines are easier to use under pressure.

"Can we pause for a second?"

"That sounded like a stereotype."

"Let's not put one person on the spot."

"Support programs are about equity."

"I do not think that joke lands well."

"No report does not mean no harm."

"I noticed what happened. Are you okay?"

"I can stay with you or help find support."

After the moment

Do not turn your response into another burden.

The aim is not to perform being a good bystander. The aim is to reduce harm, support the person affected and change the pattern that made the moment possible.

Do not ask for reassurance

Avoid making the person affected comfort you or rate your response.

Do not force a report

Offer options and support. Reporting should not remove control from the person harmed.

Do keep learning

Use First Nations-led resources. Do not rely on one student to explain racism, culture or history.
