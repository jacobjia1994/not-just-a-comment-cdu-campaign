Evidence-informed design

This is a student campaign concept, not an official CDU service.

The design draws on social and emotional wellbeing frameworks, racism and wellbeing research, university racism evidence and bystander anti-racism literature.

Racism as a wellbeing issue

AIHW/Calma et al. (2025), Kairuz et al. (2021) and Thurber et al. (2021) support the campaign's core claim that racism and everyday discrimination are linked with mental health and social and emotional wellbeing harms.

Social and emotional wellbeing

Purdie, Dudgeon and Walker (2010) support a holistic understanding of wellbeing that includes body, mind, family, kinship, community, culture, Country, spirituality and history.

Stigma, shame and depathologising practice

Tseris (2019) is used to avoid individualising distress. Walter and Baltra-Ulloa (2019) help position racism, whiteness and dominant institutional norms as the problem, not First Nations students' resilience.

University setting

The Australian Human Rights Commission's Racism@Uni study supports focusing on university spaces, belonging, safety and low reporting confidence.

Bystander design

Nelson, Dunn and Paradies (2011) support the need for recognition, responsibility, confidence, practical words and supportive peer norms before bystanders act.

Visual and cultural safety note

This public student version does not imitate Aboriginal art or use decorative Indigenous motifs. The landscape image is a CC BY-SA 3.0 photograph of Casuarina Beach by GDW.45 via Wikimedia Commons. A real CDU version should commission or license artwork from First Nations artists, with clear permission, payment, cultural review and attribution.
