Why this campaign is built this way

Racism is a wellbeing issue.

The campaign targets everyday anti-Indigenous racism because racism can shape safety, belonging, participation, shame and social and emotional wellbeing.

01

Racism is not only an attitude problem.

Anti-Indigenous racism is connected with social and emotional wellbeing, mental health, safety and participation. A mental wellbeing campaign can therefore target the conditions that create shame, distress and exclusion.

02

Everyday racism matters.

Campaigns such as Beyond Blue’s Stop. Think. Respect and the Australian Human Rights Commission’s anti-racism work challenge the idea that subtle or “casual” racism is harmless.

03

University is the right setting.

Australian university evidence shows racism affects students and staff, including First Nations students. This campaign is aimed at CDU students as a peer community, not at an abstract public.

04

Awareness needs action.

Awareness alone is weak if students do not know what to do. Bystander intervention models show the value of simple, realistic action choices.

05

The burden should not sit with First Nations students.

The campaign avoids asking First Nations students to educate others or speak for all Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples.

06

It challenges dominant stories.

The campaign challenges common stories that make racism easier to excuse: “I don’t see race,” “it was only a joke,” “they get special treatment,” and “no one reported it, so it must be fine.” These stories can shift shame and responsibility onto the person harmed.

07

The framing is depathologising.

The site does not frame First Nations identity as a risk factor or First Nations students as the problem. It locates harm in racism, silence and peer norms.
