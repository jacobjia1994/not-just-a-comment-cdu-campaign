Why this matters

Everyday anti-Indigenous racism is a wellbeing issue.

These points explain why this site focuses on everyday comments, bystander action, support and social and emotional wellbeing.

What the evidence says

Racism affects safety, belonging and wellbeing.

Use this page when you want the research behind the actions.

What you can do
AIHW/Calma et al. link systemic anti-Indigenous racism with First Nations social and emotional wellbeing and mental health.
Racism can harm wellbeing through safety, belonging, identity, culture and trust.
Treat racism as a wellbeing concern, not as a minor interpersonal disagreement.
Kairuz et al. and Thurber et al. link racism and everyday discrimination with psychological distress and wellbeing harms.
Everyday discrimination can carry cumulative harm.
Pay attention to repeated jokes, assumptions and silences before they become the norm.
Shame and stigma literature frames stigma as a power-laden social process tied to exclusion.
Racist comments can create shame and silence, not only offence.
Notice when a comment makes someone's identity, support or participation feel unsafe.
Tseris critiques anti-stigma campaigns that individualise distress.
Students harmed by racism should not be told to cope better while the room stays unchanged.
Change the peer norm. Check in. Do not make the person harmed carry the response.
Walter and Baltra-Ulloa focus attention on whiteness, colour-blindness and meritocracy.
Colour-blindness and "special treatment" talk can protect comfort while leaving racism unnamed.
Name the assumption without turning the moment into a personal attack.
AHRC Racism@Uni identifies widespread racism in Australian universities and low complaint use.
Low reporting cannot be read as proof that harm did not occur.
Check in and offer options. Do not force a formal complaint.
Bystander anti-racism research stresses recognition, responsibility, confidence and practical words.
People are more likely to act when they know what harm looks like and have words to use.
Use a short line, redirect the room or follow up after the moment.
First Nations ethical principles and self-determination caution against decorative cultural use.
Cultural safety requires permission, governance and attribution.
Do not use Aboriginal art, stories or cultural material as decoration without permission.
