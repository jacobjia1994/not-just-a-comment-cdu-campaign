Evidence-informed design

This is a student campaign concept, not an official CDU service.

Each content choice is linked to research, course theory or a mature public campaign model.

Evidence to design

The campaign is built as a micro-learning resource, not a slogan page.

The table shows how the research basis becomes page content.

Evidence
Campaign insight
Design choice
AIHW/Calma et al. link systemic anti-Indigenous racism with First Nations social and emotional wellbeing and mental health.
Racism belongs in a mental wellbeing assessment, not only a diversity campaign.
The home page names belonging, safety and social and emotional wellbeing.
Kairuz et al. and Thurber et al. link racism and everyday discrimination with psychological distress and wellbeing harms.
Everyday discrimination can carry cumulative harm.
The campaign uses five everyday campus moments rather than only extreme incidents.
SWK525 shame and stigma material frames stigma as a power-laden social process tied to exclusion.
Racist comments can create shame and silence, not only offence.
Each moment explains how a comment affects identity, support, participation or reporting.
Tseris critiques anti-stigma campaigns that individualise distress.
A strong campaign should not tell First Nations students to cope better.
The campaign targets bystanders, peer norms and support pathways.
Walter and Baltra-Ulloa focus attention on whiteness, colour-blindness and meritocracy.
Dominant student assumptions need to be named.
The moments include "I do not see race" and "special treatment".
AHRC Racism@Uni identifies widespread racism in Australian universities and low complaint use.
A university setting is justified, and low reporting cannot be read as no harm.
The Support page centres "No complaint does not mean no harm".
Bystander anti-racism research stresses recognition, responsibility, confidence and practical words.
Awareness alone is weak. Students need language and safe options.
The Act page provides steps and phrase banks.
First Nations ethical principles and self-determination caution against decorative cultural use.
Cultural safety requires permission, governance and attribution.
The visual note avoids imitating Aboriginal art and recommends commissioned First Nations artwork for real implementation.

Visual and cultural safety note

This public student version does not imitate Aboriginal art or use decorative Indigenous motifs. The landscape image is a CC BY-SA 3.0 photograph of Casuarina Beach by GDW.45 via Wikimedia Commons. A real CDU version should commission or license artwork from First Nations artists, with clear permission, payment, cultural review and attribution.

Embedded videos are from the Australian Human Rights Commission and Beyond Blue Official YouTube channels. They are used as external educational prompts and remain the property of their publishers.

Possible CDU use

This student campaign concept could be used as a QR-linked microsite in CDU orientation, Learnline, social work tutorials, group-work preparation, placement-readiness discussions or student leadership training. A real implementation would need First Nations review, CDU approval, accessibility testing and current support contacts.
