Why this matters

Small moments can carry a heavy message.

Racist jokes, “special treatment” claims, colour-blind comments and silence after harm can mark Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander identity, support needs and cultural knowledge as things to be judged. This campaign is designed for students who witness those moments and are unsure what to do next.

Social and emotional wellbeing

Racism is a wellbeing issue, not just a behaviour issue.

First Nations social and emotional wellbeing is holistic. It includes mind, body, family, kinship, community, culture, Country, spirituality and history. When racism affects safety, belonging or identity, it can become a barrier to wellbeing.

Stigma and shame

The problem is not First Nations students’ resilience.

The problem is the social process that makes people feel watched, questioned, mocked, blamed or expected to educate the room. This campaign uses a depathologising approach: it shifts responsibility toward bystanders, peer norms and institutional support.

Five days, five moments

Everyday racism often arrives as ordinary campus talk.

Each moment below is a composite educational scenario. It is not the story of any individual student.

Day 1

“It was only a joke.”

A joke can teach the room what is allowed.

Open response

Try: “Can we not make people the punchline?”

Action: Pause the moment. Name the harm. Check in privately.

Day 2

“They get special treatment.”

Equity support should not become a reason to shame someone’s place at university.

Open response

Try: “Support programs are about equity, not unfair advantage.”

Action: Challenge the myth without asking First Nations students to justify support services.

Day 3

“I do not see race.”

Colour-blindness can make racism harder to name.

Open response

Try: “Treating people fairly includes noticing how racism affects people differently.”

Action: Listen when racism is named, instead of explaining it away.

Day 4

“Can you explain the Aboriginal view?”

One student is not a cultural help desk.

Open response

Try: “Let’s not put one person on the spot.”

Action: Use First Nations-led resources. Do your own learning.

Day 5

“No one reported it, so it must be fine.”

Low reporting is not proof that racism is harmless.

Open response

Try: “People do not always report racism, even when it causes harm.”

Action: Check in. Document safely. Find support.

Support pathways

No complaint does not mean no harm.

People may stay silent because reporting can feel unsafe, pointless or exhausting. If racism occurs, support can begin with a private check-in, careful documentation, or asking a trusted staff member or support service for advice.

Evidence-informed design

This is a student campaign concept, not an official CDU service.

The design draws on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander social and emotional wellbeing frameworks, research on racism and wellbeing, the Australian Human Rights Commission’s Racism@Uni study, and bystander anti-racism literature. Real implementation would require First Nations review, current CDU pathway checks and appropriate institutional support.

Key sources: AIHW/Calma et al. (2025); Kairuz et al. (2021); Thurber et al. (2021); Australian Human Rights Commission (2026); Nelson, Dunn and Paradies (2011); Purdie, Dudgeon and Walker (2010); Tseris (2019); Walter and Baltra-Ulloa (2019).
