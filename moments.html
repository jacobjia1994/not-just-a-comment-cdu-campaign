Moment library

Everyday racism often arrives as ordinary campus talk.

These composite scenarios show how jokes, myths, colour-blindness, cultural labour, identity policing, historical denial, online racism and silence can become barriers to belonging and wellbeing.

Day 1

"It was only a joke."

What is happening

A racist joke is treated as harmless humour. The group is invited to laugh, stay quiet or move on.

Why it matters

The joke turns identity into a punchline. If no one responds, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students may be expected to absorb the discomfort so everyone else can stay comfortable.

Bystander line

"Can we not make people the punchline?" Then check in privately with anyone affected.

Reflect

What made the joke easy for the group to dismiss?

Day 2

"They get special treatment."

What is happening

Equity support is framed as unfair advantage rather than a response to unequal conditions and exclusion.

Why it matters

This can shame Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students for using support or being present at university. It also repeats a meritocracy myth that everyone starts from the same place.

Bystander line

"Support programs are about equity, not unfair advantage." Keep the focus on systems, not one student.

Reflect

Whose history disappears when support is treated as special treatment?

Day 3

"I do not see race."

What is happening

Someone presents colour-blindness as fairness and avoids talking about racism directly.

Why it matters

Colour-blindness can make racism harder to discuss. It asks people affected by racism to fit into a version of fairness that ignores unequal treatment.

Bystander line

"Treating people fairly includes noticing how racism affects people differently."

Reflect

Who benefits when race becomes too uncomfortable to name?

Day 4

"Can you explain the Aboriginal view?"

What is happening

One student is asked to speak for diverse peoples, histories, communities and political positions.

Why it matters

The question may sound curious, but it can turn a student into a cultural help desk. Participation should not depend on unpaid education work.

Bystander line

"Let's not put one person on the spot. We can use First Nations-led resources and do the reading."

Reflect

What could the group learn before asking one person to carry the topic?

Day 5

"No one reported it, so it must be fine."

What is happening

Low complaint numbers or silence are used as proof that racism is not happening.

Why it matters

People may avoid reporting because the process feels unsafe, tiring, unclear or unlikely to help. Silence can mean risk, not absence of harm.

Bystander line

"People do not always report racism, even when it causes harm." Check in, document safely and seek advice.

Reflect

What would make reporting safer and more trusted?

Day 6

"They get everything for free."

What is happening

A broad stereotype is used to resent Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people and dismiss unequal access, exclusion and need.

Why it matters

The comment can make students feel suspected, undeserving or personally responsible for explaining public policy and history.

Bystander line

"That is a stereotype. Support is not proof that people get everything for free."

Reflect

What is being ignored when support is treated as resentment material?

Day 7

"You do not look Aboriginal."

What is happening

Someone treats identity as something they can inspect, measure or approve from appearance.

Why it matters

Identity policing can shame people, invite intrusive questions and reproduce colonial ideas about authenticity.

Bystander line

"Identity is not something we judge by appearance."

Reflect

Why do some people feel entitled to test another person's identity?

Day 8

"Why are we still talking about colonisation?"

What is happening

History is framed as old, irrelevant or divisive, even when it shapes current systems and trust.

Why it matters

Making history optional can make present-day racism harder to understand in health, education, child protection, justice and social work.

Bystander line

"We are talking about it because history still shapes people's safety, health and access to services."

Reflect

Who benefits when history is treated as too uncomfortable to discuss?

Day 9

"That family is just difficult."

What is happening

In a placement or practice discussion, a family or client is judged through deficit language without considering culture, history, power or service mistrust.

Why it matters

Practice settings can turn stereotypes into decisions. Students need to slow down assumptions before they become professional judgement.

Bystander line

"Can we check whether we are making a cultural assumption here and take this to supervision?"

Reflect

What information would you need before describing someone as "difficult"?

Day 10

"I cannot pronounce that, so I will just skip it."

What is happening

A name, language word, Country name or community name is treated as optional because it is unfamiliar.

Why it matters

Avoiding or joking about names can make language and place feel like an inconvenience rather than something worth respecting.

Bystander line

"Let's slow down and learn how to say it properly."

Reflect

What does it signal when a room tries to pronounce some words but not others?

Day 11

"Everyone struggles. Why make it about racism?"

What is happening

A universal statement is used to flatten a specific harm and avoid naming racism.

Why it matters

People can share hardship while racism still adds a distinct burden. Treating all struggle as the same can silence the extra harm.

Bystander line

"Everyone can struggle, and racism can still create a specific harm we need to name."

Reflect

When does "everyone" language help, and when does it erase difference?

Day 12

A racist meme appears in the group chat.

What is happening

Online racism is shared as humour, and silence can make it look acceptable to the group.

Why it matters

Group chats are still learning spaces. A student should not have to leave the chat or absorb racism to stay in the course network.

Bystander line

"I do not want racist memes in this chat. Please delete it."

Reflect

What does silence teach in an online group?
