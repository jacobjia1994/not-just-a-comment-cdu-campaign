It is not one comment

Anti-Indigenous racism works through patterns.

One joke, one myth or one silence may look small. Repeated across classrooms, group chats, placements and services, it can tell Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students where they are expected to fit, explain, endure or stay quiet.

What it can look like

Racism does not always announce itself as hate.

It can sound casual, reasonable, curious or even polite. That is why students need to recognise the pattern, not just the most obvious slurs.

01

Jokes and casual talk

Racist humour, stereotypes, nicknames and "just joking" comments make identity available for ridicule while asking the person targeted not to react.

02

Deficit assumptions

Comments about alcohol, parenting, attendance, intelligence or "not valuing education" can turn structural inequality into a judgement about Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people.

03

Fairness myths

"Special treatment" talk frames equity support as unfair advantage and ignores histories of exclusion, ongoing discrimination and unequal starting points.

04

Colour-blind denial

"I do not see race" can sound inclusive, but it can also make racism unsayable and ask people to pretend unequal treatment is not happening.

05

Cultural load

One student may be expected to explain culture, represent all First Nations people, correct everyone, educate the group and stay calm while doing it.

06

Questioning identity

"You do not look Aboriginal" or "how much Aboriginal are you?" turns identity into something others feel entitled to inspect or approve.

07

History made optional

Colonisation, land, policy violence and racism are treated as old or irrelevant, even when they shape health, education, child protection, justice and trust today.

08

Online racism

Memes, group-chat jokes, comment threads and "debate" posts can spread racism quickly while making the person affected choose between speaking up and staying safe.

09

Institutional minimising

When low reporting is treated as proof that racism is rare, the focus shifts away from fear, distrust, exhaustion and whether complaints feel safe enough to use.

Why it harms wellbeing

Racism changes the room.

It can make students monitor themselves, withdraw, avoid services, doubt whether support is safe, or carry the extra work of protecting other people's comfort.

Safety

"Will I be targeted if I speak? Will staff take this seriously? Will reporting make things worse?"

Belonging

"Am I a full participant here, or someone whose place is questioned, joked about or conditional?"

Burden

"Do I have to educate the room, absorb the comment, manage the conflict and stay calm?"

Trust

"If people minimise this comment, will they also minimise harm in placements, services or professional practice?"

What changes the pattern

The aim is not a perfect speech. The aim is less harm.

A useful response makes the pattern visible, reduces the burden on the person targeted and gives the room a different norm to follow.

Name what happened

"That sounded like a stereotype." "That comment puts one person on the spot." "No report does not mean no harm."

Move the burden

Do not ask the Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander student to explain, defend, translate or reassure. Use First Nations-led resources and do the reading.

Protect choice

Check in privately. Offer support and options. Do not force a report, retell the person's story or turn your response into another performance they must manage.

Change the peer norm

If the group learns that racist jokes pass without response, the pattern strengthens. If one person interrupts it calmly, others get permission to act too.

Bring in staff when needed

Some situations are not safe for one student to handle alone. A tutor, unit coordinator, supervisor or student support service may need to be involved.

Keep learning after the moment

Anti-racism is not finished after one sentence. It includes how people read, cite, listen, supervise, design services and share responsibility.
